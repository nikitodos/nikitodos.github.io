Ikarus-Y Development

Regarding Ikarus-X, Ikarus-Y represents a pioneering concept in advanced SSTO spacecraft design. Initially, the plan involved adapting the original Ikarus-X blueprint by removing the BLDC propellers and integrating propulsion technology akin to that found in systems like this from Reaction Engines®. However, upon completion of the design phase, it became evident that the energy demands were prohibitively high. In response, I am spearheading the development of a revolutionary new engine called Skyraker. A groundbreaking aircraft, built upon the framework of Ikarus-Y, will serve as the testbed for this innovative propulsion system. Project HyperLander

Ensuring performance, not just completion.

From Malaga to Auckland in just 55 minutes

The first crewed airborne TSTS transportation system ever.

As part of my Launch Systems course at Politecnico di Milano, I co-led a multidisciplinary team to design an air-launched suborbital transportation system capable of carrying 6 passengers across ~20,000 km via ballistic trajectory. The challenge? Achieving ±20 km landing accuracy while maintaining military-grade operational flexibility and covert launch capability.

We selected the Scaled Composites Stratolaunch "Roc" as our carrier aircraft, paired with a winged Pegasus-XL-style rocket powered by LOX/LCH₄ propulsion. The engines are scaled versions of SpaceX's flight-proven Raptor 2, ensuring high TRL (Technology Readiness Level > 6) and proven performance heritage.

There were several critical areas under my direct supervision: the complete propulsion system design, including engine scaling logic derived from Raptor 2 architecture; structural design and stress analysis under extreme flight loads; the "Sirius" crew capsule derived from NASA's 2005 ESAS Orion design, integrated with its Launch Abort System (LAS); and the overall vehicle layout, mass distribution, and graphics.

The system reaches hypersonic velocity and completes the antipodal journey through a precision-controlled ballistic arc. The Sirius capsule, equipped with a triple-parachute recovery system, ensures safe landing within the required accuracy envelope. Every kilogram mattered—every design decision was a balancing act between performance, safety, and operational readiness. Maya

My first real industrial project.

The definitive high-performance flying boat, on an human scale.

A mix of sports, arts and ingenuity.

The UniPV Sailing Team, founded in April 2021, embodies the passion for sailing among students at the University of Pavia. The mission is to merge academic knowledge with practical experiences, actively engaging in the internationally recognized foiling week. At the core of the efforts lies the design and construction of foiling moth boats, with a focus on innovation and eco-sustainability. The group's first creation, Svelta, was crafted in accordance with the SuMoth Challenge guidelines.

Looking ahead, I am leading the work on Maya, a next-generation foiling moth set to debut for the Salone Nautico di Venezia 2025. Compared to the past, Maya's design marks a clear departure. Heavily inspired by the NASA X-38 Crew Return Vehicle, the design features a pair of smal wings, consisting of six glassfiber panels mounted on an aluminum grid docked to a main frame. These, along with the glassfiber aeroderivative frame, form the load-bearing structure, which bears a much closer resemblance to an aircraft than a traditional boat.

To simplify transport and rigging, the non-structural parts, such as the external flax fiber skin and bow, can be removed from the main frame, as well as the wings. With a pair of lightweight alloy bars, each bar is held fixed in the sailing position as if they were all in one piece. Finally, an innovative flight control system allows for optimal wing profile calibration regardless of the availability of electrical power sources.

Starseeker Modular Telescope

This is how it all started...

What am I able to do with my theoretical notions?

My personal JWST: Starseeker.

At the age of 15, I embarked on a journey at Nautical High School where I delved into the intricacies of celestial navigation. It was there that I discovered the perfect avenue to fuel my passion for Astronomy and Space Exploration. Serendipitously, a friend who owned ships offered me access to his machinery and abundant aluminum resources, igniting the inception of an ambitious project.

Having previously contributed to the restoration of a 30-cm Celestron® telescope from the 1980s, I repurposed its mechanical framework for my endeavor. Drawing inspiration from the groundbreaking James Webb Space Telescope, I channeled all my skills into crafting the optics. My vision was to create an amateur telescope infused with innovations borrowed from the aerospace industry, aimed at fostering a deeper comprehension of these marvels.
